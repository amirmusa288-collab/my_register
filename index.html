How to Use My Register — A Digital Notebook That Works Like a Real One

Most note-taking apps ask you to create folders, tags, and endless nested pages until your notes are technically organized but practically impossible to find. My Register takes the opposite approach. It works the way a physical register or accounts book has worked for generations: you open it, you find a free page, you write something down, and you remember the page number. That's it. No folders, no sign-up, no cloud account — just a book that lives entirely on your own device.

Opening the Register

When you first arrive, you'll see the register closed, sitting in the middle of the screen exactly like a real notebook waiting on a desk. Tap or click anywhere on it to open it. The first thing you'll see is the Index — a list of every page you've already written something on, along with the short label you gave it, plus a grid of all 100 numbered pages so you can see at a glance which ones are still empty.

Writing By Hand or By Keyboard

Open any page and you'll find two ways to write on it. The Type mode gives you a proper keyboard text area, styled like ruled paper, where you can type in either English or Urdu — just tap the EN or اردو button at the top and the page will switch its font and writing direction automatically, so Urdu text flows right-to-left in a proper Nastaliq-style font instead of looking like a browser default. The Hand mode turns the page into a small canvas where you can write or draw using your finger on a touchscreen, or your mouse on a laptop, exactly like putting a pen to paper. You can change the ink color, switch between a fine or bold pen, use the eraser, or clear the page and start again.

Turning Pages Like a Real Book

This is the detail that makes My Register feel different from a typical app. Instead of a boring instant page switch, turning a page plays a short animation of the page physically lifting and flipping to the other side, along with a soft paper-turning sound, just like flicking through a real register. You can turn pages using the arrow buttons at the top of the screen, or simply swipe — dragging your finger (or your mouse, if you're on a laptop) from left to right moves you forward through the register, page after page, and dragging from right to left brings you back.

Giving Each Page a Label

At the bottom of every page there's a small field called the Index Label. This is the single most useful habit to build when you start using My Register. Whatever you just wrote on that page — a person's phone number, a list of today's expenses, an idea you don't want to forget — type a short two or three word label describing it, like "Ali's number" or "July grocery list." That label is what shows up back on the Index page, next to the page number, so months later you don't have to flip through pages one by one. You just glance at the Index, see the label, and know exactly which page number to jump to.

Doing the Math — With Any Currency

Because a register is so often used to keep accounts, every page in Type mode has a small currency calculator built in. Tap the Currency Calc button, choose from Pakistani Rupees, Indian Rupees, US Dollars, Euros, British Pounds, Saudi Riyals, UAE Dirhams, or Japanese Yen, enter two amounts, and pick add, subtract, multiply, or divide. The result — along with the full working — can be inserted straight into your page as a written line, so your calculation becomes part of the permanent record on that page, not something you did separately and then had to copy over by hand.

Jumping Straight to a Page

If you already know the page number you're looking for — because you memorized it the way people always have with real registers — the Index screen has a simple "Jump to Page" box. Type in any number from 1 to 100 and you'll be taken straight there, without having to flip through everything in between.

Your Data Stays With You

Nothing you write in My Register is uploaded anywhere. Every page, whether typed or hand-drawn, is saved directly on your own phone or computer the moment you write it, and it's still there the next time you open the register — even without an internet connection. There's no login because there's no account to log into. The trade-off is that if you clear your browser's data or switch to a different device, your register won't follow you automatically, so if you're keeping something important, it's worth being mindful of that the same way you'd be careful with a physical notebook you don't want to lose.

When the Register Is Full

A register only has 100 pages, on purpose — just like a real one. When every page has something written on it, you'll see a message inviting you to start a new volume, exactly the way anyone who has filled up a physical accounts book simply reaches for the next one and keeps going, remembering which volume held which year or which set of records.

A Few Habits Worth Building

My Register works best when you treat it a little like the real thing rather than a normal app. Try not to jump straight to a random empty page every time — use the "+ New Entry" button on the Index screen and it will automatically find and open the next free page for you, in order, the same way you'd naturally move forward through a physical notebook rather than skipping around it. If you're recording something you might need again, spend the extra five seconds filling in the Index Label before you move on; it costs almost nothing in the moment and saves real time later. And if you switch between Hand and Type mode on the same page, remember they're independent — a page can hold either handwriting or typed text depending on which mode you were in last, so pick the one that suits what you're writing before you start.

Who This Is For

My Register isn't trying to replace a full accounting app or a note-taking suite with reminders and cloud sync. It's built for the smaller, everyday moments a physical register has always been used for — jotting down someone's phone number when you don't have paper handy, writing a quick account of what you spent today, keeping a short list you'll want to check later, or simply having somewhere private to write in your own language without opening an account first. If that sounds like the kind of notebook you used to keep in a drawer, this is the same idea, just carried in your pocket.
